Here's the underlying playbook — the same rules apply no matter whose resume it is:
1. ATS parses text, not design.
Multi-column layouts, tables, text boxes, icons, and headers/footers often get scrambled or dropped by parsing software because ATS reads left-to-right, top-to-bottom in a single stream. A resume that looks clean visually can parse as garbled nonsense. Always: single column, no tables, no graphics.
2. Section headers must be literal, not clever.
Use "Experience," "Education," "Skills" — not "My Journey" or "What I Bring." ATS software matches against a known set of standard headers to sort your content into the right fields.
3. Contact info goes in the body, not the header/footer.
Many ATS parsers skip header/footer regions entirely. Name, email, phone should sit as plain text at the top of the main content.
4. Lead every bullet with an action verb.
"Developed," "Refactored," "Implemented" > "Was responsible for" or "Helped with." Action verbs read as ownership and are what both ATS relevance scoring and human recruiters scan for first.
5. Quantify wherever the truth allows.
"Reduced load time by 30%," "85% test coverage" — numbers catch the eye and signal impact. (Never invent numbers — only use ones you can actually back up.)
6. Keyword-match to the job description, if you have one.
ATS scoring is largely keyword overlap between your resume and the job posting. If you paste a JD next time, I'll mirror its exact terminology (e.g., if they say "state management," don't just write "Redux" — include both).
7. Group skills into labeled categories.
"Languages / Frameworks / Tools" instead of one long comma-dump — easier for both ATS field extraction and recruiter skimming.
8. Consistent, unambiguous date formatting.
"Jan 2024 – Jun 2024" everywhere, not a mix of "1/24," "January 2024," "Present."
9. Cut redundancy, fit one page.
Recruiters spend ~6-8 seconds on first pass. Every repeated phrase or filler bullet ("passionate team player") pushes out something that could've been a real accomplishment.
